## Paper B: "The Geometric Atom: Deriving the Fine Structure Constant from Lattice Helicity"
**File:** `geometric_atom_symplectic_revision.pdf` (12 pages)

### Scope
- **Two-manifold coupling theory**
- Electron lattice (from Paper A) + photon gauge fiber
- Electromagnetic coupling via symplectic impedance
- **Predicts value consistent with α**

### Key Claims (Revised)
1. **Bridge to Paper A:** Explicitly builds on single-particle model, adding photon degrees of freedom
2. **Symplectic impedance:** Ratio $\kappa_n = S_n/P_n$ converges to value consistent with $1/\alpha = 137.036$ at $n=5$
3. **Geometric mean ansatz:** Helical pitch formula $\delta = \sqrt{\pi \langle L_\pm \rangle} = 3.081$ (0.15% from required value)
4. **Helicity signature:** Helical geometry (spin-1) provides better agreement than circular (spin-0)

### Major Revisions Made
1. **Abstract:** Softened claims—now states "consistent with" rather than "proves" or "derives"; calls pitch formula an "ansatz"
2. **New Section:** "From Single-Particle Geometry to Coupled Manifolds"
   - Summarizes results from Paper A
   - Explains Paper A was intentionally single-particle
   - States α cannot appear without two manifolds
   - Frames this work as hypothesis testing
3. **New Section:** "Consistency with the Single-Particle Lattice"
   - Explicitly reconciles both papers
   - Lists what Paper A provided vs. what this paper adds
   - Acknowledges limitations and open questions
   - Emphasizes geometric mean formula is ansatz, not theorem
4. **Calibrated language throughout:**
   - "Exact match" → "convergence to" / "consistent with"
   - "Proves" → "suggests" / "indicates"
   - "Required" → "consistent with"

## Summary Statement

**The fine structure constant α emerges as a geometric impedance ratio when two distinct quantum manifolds—the electron paraboloid lattice (SO(4,2) symmetry) and the photon helical gauge fiber (U(1) symmetry)—are coupled via symplectic action. Paper A establishes the single-particle electron foundation. Paper B introduces photon degrees of freedom and tests whether the impedance ratio converges to 1/α, finding agreement to 0.15% at the n=5 topological resonance using a geometric mean impedance matching ansatz. While striking numerically, this framework remains exploratory and requires deeper group-theoretic justification before it can be considered a first-principles derivation. Together, these papers suggest that fundamental coupling constants may be topological invariants of multi-manifold quantum systems.**
